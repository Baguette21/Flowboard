# Product

## Register

product

## Users

PlanThing is for people who want to grow a plan into something real: solo users, small teams, project collaborators, students, builders, creators, and anyone who needs a flexible workspace rather than a fixed project-management doctrine. The app should not assume one rigid workflow. Boards, notes, drawings, labels, assignments, and customization should let each person shape the workspace around their own way of planning.

## Product Purpose

PlanThing helps users capture, organize, and advance work across boards, tasks, notes, and visual canvases. It exists to give plans enough structure to move forward without making the user fight a heavyweight system. Success means users can quickly create a board or note, understand what needs attention, collaborate in real time, and adapt the workspace as their plan becomes more complex.

## Brand Personality

Calm, clean, tactile, and fast. The product should feel like a focused working surface: quiet enough for planning, responsive enough for daily use, and tactile enough that cards, tabs, notes, and actions feel concrete. Notion is a useful baseline for clarity and flexibility, but PlanThing should have its own warmer, more task-forward identity.

## Anti-references

PlanThing must not feel like corporate Jira, a generic Notion clone, a neon AI dashboard, a childish task app, a cluttered Trello board, or over-designed startup SaaS. It should avoid rigid enterprise ceremony, decorative AI visual tropes, toy-like styling, dense unchecked clutter, and empty marketing polish that slows down real work.

## Design Principles

1. Shape around the user's plan, not the other way around. Customization and flexible views are part of the product promise.
2. Keep the surface calm under load. As boards, notes, labels, and assignments grow, hierarchy should reduce noise instead of adding more decoration.
3. Make work feel tangible. Cards, notes, tabs, and controls should have clear affordances, responsive feedback, and stable spatial behavior.
4. Be fast in the user's hands. Common actions should be obvious, keyboard-friendly where practical, and free of unnecessary ceremony.
5. Borrow clarity, not identity. Notion can inform the baseline, but PlanThing should not become a Notion imitation.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA. The product should support reduced-motion preferences, color-blind safe labels and statuses, visible focus states, keyboard-first workflows for core actions, readable contrast in light and dark themes, and interface copy that does not rely on color alone to communicate state.
